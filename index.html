The art that sells
itself is the art
people feel first.

There are two completely different audiences on social media: people who want to learn ceramics, and people who want to own beautiful objects. This guide is for reaching the second one — the art buyer, the collector, the person who walks past your work and feels something they can't name yet.

Story
is the product
Desire

World

The Core Distinction

Two feeds.
Two worlds.

When you talk about clay, processes, and mistakes — you attract artists. They admire you, they learn from you, and some will eventually buy your course. That's a real audience with real value.

But the person who buys your work — who places it on their mantle, who gifts it, who messages you at midnight asking if that piece is still available — they don't care about firing temperatures. They care about how the piece makes them feel. They are buying a piece of your world into theirs.

Reaching them requires a completely different language. Not problem-solving. Not hacks. Poetry. Presence. Point of view.

Attracts Artists
"3 mistakes beginners make"
"How I fix cracks in clay"
"Technique breakdown: coiling"
"Air-dry vs. fired: comparison"
"Tools I use in my studio"
Attracts Buyers
"This piece took six attempts to exist"
"I make things that hold the weight of mornings"
"On slowness in a world that wants fast"
"What your hands touch every day matters"
"This is the feeling I'm always chasing"
01
Your Work Carries a Philosophy

You believe that what surrounds us shapes how we live. That a handmade object — something with a maker's fingerprint still in it — changes the quality of a person's everyday life in ways they feel but can't always explain. That belief is your brand. Say it, again and again, in different ways.

"I don't just make ceramics. I make the bowl that starts your morning differently."
02
The Collector Buys the Story First

Art buyers have always purchased narrative alongside object. When you share the emotion behind a piece — the memory it came from, the mood you were in, the feeling you were reaching for — you give collectors something to hold onto beyond the clay itself. That story travels with the piece. It's what makes it worth five times more than a store-bought equivalent.

"She asked me what this piece was about. I said: the Sunday feeling of not wanting the weekend to end."
03
Desire is Built Through Repetition

Nobody buys from a stranger. Your buyer watches 20 posts before they DM. They need to know who you are, what you believe, and how you see the world. Each post is one more thread in the tapestry. You are not selling a piece — you are inviting someone into a worldview they didn't know they needed.

"By the time they ask 'is this still available?' — they already know the answer is yes."
The Framework

Three layers of story
that create desire.

I
🫀
Who You Are

Your beliefs, your way of seeing, your relationship to slowness, craft, beauty, and time. This is the layer that makes people feel like they know you — and want to.

What you believe making things by hand does for a person
Why you chose clay over every other material
What "professional" means to you as an artist
The moment you knew this was your life
What you notice that others walk past
II
🏺
The Piece's World

Not technique. Feeling. Origin. Mood. Give each piece a story that predates the purchase. The collector is not buying clay — they are buying a specific feeling made permanent.

The emotion you were processing when you made this
Where the form came from (memory, nature, accident)
What kind of home it belongs in
The light it catches, the feeling it sits with
What a person's life looks like with this in it
III
🌀
The Viewer's Reflection

This is the most powerful layer — and the least used. Write to the feeling the buyer already has inside them. Name something they've felt but never said. They'll think you made the piece for them.

"For the people who eat breakfast slowly on purpose"
"If you believe the objects around you matter — this is for you"
"For the homes that choose things with intention"
"For anyone who knows the difference between fast and made"
"This piece is for a specific kind of quiet"
Content Types

What to actually post
to reach the collector.

🎬
Reel · 15–30 sec
The Slow Reveal

Open on a finished piece in natural light. Slow rotation. Close-up on texture — a thumb imprint, a glaze edge, where two forms meet. No voiceover. Just music and presence. Then one line of text, late in the video: your belief about what the object does.

Caption to pair with it "Some things don't need to be useful. They just need to exist in your space and make you feel like yourself."
✍️
Carousel · 4–6 slides
The Origin Story

First slide: the finished piece, beautiful photo. Slides 2–5: the story behind it. Not technique — emotion. What were you thinking, feeling, reaching for? Final slide: piece again with purchase information or a question that invites response.

Slide 1 opening line "This one took three weeks and I almost gave up twice. Here's what it's actually about."
🎥
Reel · 8–12 sec
The 1-Second Cut Sequence

NOT a technique tutorial. A sensory poem. Film process close-ups: hands pressing clay, glaze being poured, a piece spinning slowly. Rapid cuts at beat drops. The goal is hypnotic atmosphere — the feeling of making, not the instruction of it. Buyers find this meditative, beautiful, irresistible.

Caption to pair with it "There's a reason you can't look away. Made slowly. Meant to last."
🌅
Single Post or Story
The Still Life

A beautiful, considered photograph. Your piece in a context that shows how it lives. Morning light, a tablecloth, a plant. This is visual world-building — you're showing not just the object but the life it belongs to. Collectors are buying a version of the life they want.

Caption direction Write about the morning. The light. The coffee. Only mention the piece at the end, or not at all — let the image do it.
💬
Caption-Heavy Post
The Belief Statement

One striking image. A long caption that reads like an essay — your philosophy of making, your relationship to beauty, slowness, or intention. This content attracts people who think. People who buy things carefully. It positions you as an artist with a worldview, not a seller with a product.

Opening line example "I started making ceramics because I wanted something in my hands to be slow, in a life that only asked for fast."
🌀
Stories · Casual
The Studio Day

Not tutorials. Not mistakes. Moments. The light hitting the shelf at a certain hour. A piece drying you're nervous about. The music you're making to. These small windows into your world build the intimacy that converts followers into buyers. Collectors want to feel close to the maker.

Tone direction Think journal entry, not content. "Today I made four pieces and kept one." That's enough.
She's not a beginner.
She's a buyer. She lives with intention. She chooses slowly.
Your Art Buyer

Know the person
you're talking to.

Who she is
She's 28–45. She earns well and spends thoughtfully. Her home is curated — not from a catalog, but from places she's been and things she's found. She buys art the way she buys wine: slowly, with attention, for meaning not status.
What she believes
That objects carry energy. That handmade matters. That surrounding yourself with beautiful, intentional things is a form of self-respect — not luxury. She would never explain this to people who don't already understand it.
What she needs from you
Not education. Recognition. She needs to see herself reflected in your work and your words. She needs to feel like you made this for someone exactly like her — before she even asks the price.
What makes her buy
A story she can't stop thinking about. An image she's already saved twice. A caption that named something she's felt for years. The moment she thinks "I need this in my home" — the price is secondary.
What makes her leave
Feeling like she's in a classroom. Discount codes. Urgency tactics. Anything that breaks the aesthetic spell of your world. She's buying art — treat her like it.
Voice in Practice

Caption templates
that create longing.

The Belief
"I believe the things you touch every day shape the person you become. That's why I make objects for the moments that happen before anyone's watching — the early morning, the slow dinner, the quiet hour."
Articulates your worldview. Attracts buyers who share it. Doesn't sell — but creates the desire to own something made by this specific mind.
The Piece Story
"This one came from a week when I needed to hold something heavy. I made it dense on purpose. Some pieces are built to just sit there and hold the room steady. This is one of those."
Gives the object emotional weight before the price tag appears. The collector now owns a feeling, not just a shape.
The Invitation
"For the homes that choose things with intention. For the people who know there's a difference between having something and living with it. This piece is for a specific kind of quiet — I think you'll recognize it."
Speaks directly to the buyer's identity. "I think you'll recognize it" is one of the most powerful phrases in collector marketing.
The Moment
"It was 7am and the light was doing something to the shelf I hadn't seen before. I didn't move. I just watched. This is what was on it."
Drops the buyer into your world without explaining it. Simple, sensory, intimate. The image carries the rest.
The Naming
"There's a feeling between finishing something and letting it go. Like it was yours for a moment — all the way yours — and now it's ready to be someone else's entirely. I love that part."
Names an emotion the buyer hasn't heard named before. This builds trust, intimacy, and makes them feel seen by you — which is exactly why they'll buy from you.
The Why
"I started making ceramics to prove that slow was still possible. That you could make something that took weeks and had no algorithm and no comment section and was just — real. I'm still proving it."
Origin story + belief + positioning against the digital world. Resonates deeply with buyers who feel overwhelmed by fast culture and are drawn to the antidote.
Visual Language

Your feed is
a world, not a gallery.

☀️
Natural Light Always

Morning or golden hour. Soft shadows. Never studio flash. The warmth of real light is what makes handmade objects look handmade — which is exactly what your buyer is paying for.

🔍
Extreme Close-Ups

The thumb imprint. The glaze edge where two colors meet. The texture your buyer will feel before they've ever touched it. Close-ups create desire — they let the viewer imagine holding it.

🤍
White Background as Power Move

A clean white background is editorial, not commercial — it's how galleries and luxury brands present objects. When the piece is strong enough, nothing else is needed. Alternate between styled context and pure white; both serve the work. The key is intention, not avoidance.

🎨
Palette Consistency

Your feed should feel like a single room. Earthy, warm, considered. A consistent visual palette is what makes a buyer feel safe enough to trust your aesthetic with their own home.

The Rhythm

A collector-focused
weekly content flow.

Monday
Reel
Slow reveal of a piece. Close-up texture, natural light. Music + one belief line. No voiceover.
Tuesday
Stories
A studio window. What you're working on. No explanation — just presence. 2–3 frames.
Wednesday
Carousel
Origin story of a piece. 4–5 slides. Photo → emotion → memory → meaning → available or "already gone."
Thursday
Reel
1-second process cuts. Sensory and atmospheric — not instructional. Hypnotic rhythm, beat-drop edits.
Stories
Ask a question or share something personal. Low stakes, high warmth.
Friday
Single Post
Still life photograph. Long caption — belief statement, essay, or naming of a feeling. Let it breathe.
Saturday
Stories
What you're doing this weekend. Real, quiet, personal. The human behind the work.
Sunday
Rest + Batch
Film everything for next week. One good light source. One hour. The content you need is already in your studio.
